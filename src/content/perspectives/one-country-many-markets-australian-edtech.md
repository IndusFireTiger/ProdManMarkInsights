The Australian EdTech market is the kind of number that makes a pitch deck sing:
roughly USD 3.9 billion in 2024, projected to about USD 7.4 billion by 2033, a
7.3% compound growth rate, tailwinds from a $1.2 billion national Digital Economy
Strategy. Read that paragraph and you'd be forgiven for drawing up a plan to sell
"to Australian schools" as though that were a single, addressable thing.

It isn't. And the most useful insight in the whole landscape is structural, not
financial: **Australia is one country but many markets.** It is a federation of
buyers, fractured along two lines at once — by *jurisdiction* (a national
curriculum, but eight states and territories that each control how and when it's
implemented) and by *funding model* (government and non-government schools that
operate, procure, and decide almost nothing alike). When you map the companies
actually succeeding in this market against that structure, a strategy lesson
falls straight out: **in a structurally fragmented market, the wedge beats the
platform.** The org chart of the market *is* the go-to-market plan. Get the
structure right and the product strategy is half-written; ignore it and the best
product in the category will stall in procurement.

## Fracture one: the federal seam

On paper, Australian education is unified. A national curriculum from ACARA, a
ten-level Australian Qualifications Framework standardising credentials since
1995, national reporting and assessment. A product person new to the market reads
"national curriculum" and hears "one integration, one content alignment, one
sale."

Then the seam shows. The national framework sets *what* is taught; the states and
territories decide *how, when, and on what timeline* it's adopted — along with
school registration, attendance rules, even leaving ages. Victoria runs its own
"Digital Technologies" emphasis and an Education Technology Consortium; New South
Wales puts AU$328 million into a schools fibre rollout; South Australia
restructured its year levels; each jurisdiction funds, procures, and prioritises
on its own clock. The practical consequence for an EdTech company is unglamorous
but decisive: *a national content alignment gets you in the room, but you sell
state by state.* The reference customer in Victoria does not automatically open
the door in Queensland. The pilot that worked in one department's procurement
framework has to be re-run in the next.

This is why a national splash is usually the wrong opening move here and a
phased, jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction entry is usually the right one — each state
a separate beachhead with its own proof points, its own departmental
relationships, its own funding cycle. The federal structure doesn't just shape
distribution; it sets the *tempo* of the whole expansion.

## Fracture two: the funding line that splits the market in two

The deeper fracture is financial, and it's the one most outsiders miss entirely.
Australian schooling runs on a multi-source funding model, and the gap between
its two halves is stark. In 2022–23, total government spending averaged about
**$24,860 per student in government schools** against roughly **$14,213 in
non-government schools** — and for independent schools, government sources covered
only around $13,080, close to half what a government-school student attracts, with
parents making up the rest.

That gap isn't trivia; it produces **two different customers who happen to wear
the same uniform.**

| | Government schools | Non-government schools |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Money** | Higher per-student public funding; system-funded | Heavy reliance on fees and parental contribution |
| **Who decides** | Centralised, departmental, system-wide procurement | Autonomous; individual school leadership |
| **What they optimise for** | Equitable scale, cost-effectiveness, curriculum and infrastructure fit | Differentiation, perceived value, a distinctive offer to attract families |
| **What sells** | Integration, compliance, scalability, alignment to system priorities | Pedagogical edge, experience, a story a parent will pay for |
| **Sales motion** | Slow, evidence-led, relationship with the department | Faster, value-led, relationship with the principal |

Sell the same product to both with the same motion and you'll lose both. The
government-school buyer is running a procurement designed to serve a whole
population equitably at defensible cost; they want to know it scales, integrates
with what they already run, and aligns to system mandates. The non-government
buyer has discretion and is competing for enrolments; they'll pay for something
that makes their school visibly better, and they'll decide far closer to the
classroom. Same category, opposite go-to-market. **The funding line is a
segmentation line**, and the companies that grow here have a deliberate answer to
*which side they're selling to* — and usually build the product and the pitch
around one of them first.

## The pattern the company list confirms: a market of wedges

Now look at who actually populates this market, and a striking regularity
appears. Almost no one is "an Australian EdTech platform." Nearly everyone is a
**specialist who owns one wedge** — one subject, one sector, one workflow:

- **By subject:** Matific and Mathspace and Maths Pathway in mathematics; 3P
  Learning in literacy and numeracy via Mathletics and Reading Eggs. Not "K-12
  learning" — *maths*, mostly, done deeply.
- **By sector:** Skillsoft and Go1 in corporate L&D; Typsy in hospitality
  training; Practera in higher-ed work-integrated learning; SimConverse in
  *healthcare communication training specifically* — a wedge inside a wedge.
- **By workflow:** Janison and Cadmus in assessment; Octopus BI in education
  analytics; EdSmart in school administration; Campion in resources and
  logistics. Each owns a slice of the operational stack rather than the whole
  thing.
- **By technology niche:** JigSpace and Curiious and Makers Empire in immersive
  and 3D/AR learning, where the wedge is the *modality*.

This is not an accident of an immature market. It's the rational response to a
fragmented one. When the buying surface is split by jurisdiction *and* by funding
model *and* by education sector (early childhood, K-12, VET, higher ed,
corporate), a horizontal "everything platform" has to win many different sales,
to many different buyers, with many different motions, all at once — and it tends
to be out-executed in each niche by a specialist who only has to win one. The
wedge isn't a starter strategy you outgrow; in a market shaped like this one,
it's frequently the winning strategy full stop. Depth in a defensible niche beats
breadth across an ungovernable one.

> The mistake isn't being small. It's being *broad* in a market that pays for
> being *deep*. Pick the wedge you can own — a subject, a sector, a workflow, a
> jurisdiction — and own it completely before you earn the right to the next one.

The two companies on the list that *do* span breadth are instructive exceptions
that prove the rule. Go1 sells breadth, but as an *aggregator* — one subscription
across 250-plus providers — so its wedge is the integration and the catalogue,
not the content. Skillsoft spans leadership, tech, and compliance, but only
inside the corporate-L&D sector, where the buyer is a single enterprise function,
not a fractured public system. Even the "broad" players are narrow on the axis
that actually matters.

## The two pains that cut across every wedge

If the market is fragmented, is there anything *universal* an EdTech company can
anchor to? Yes — two structural pains run through every jurisdiction and both
sides of the funding line, and they're where the durable demand sits:

1. **Teacher workload and supply.** The sector is short an estimated 4,100
   secondary teachers across 2021–25; initial-teacher-education commencements fell
   8% and completions 17% over 2017–2020; and a remarkable **92% of teachers
   report not having enough time to plan lessons** — all while enrolments climb.
   This is a national, bipartisan, funded emergency (the National Teacher
   Workforce Action Plan, fee cuts for education degrees). Any product that
   credibly *gives a teacher time back* — automating admin, grading, content
   generation — is selling into a pain every buyer already admits to. The framing
   that wins is force-multiplier, not replacement.
2. **Equity of access.** Despite decades of investment, outcomes still gap sharply
   by socioeconomic status, by Indigenous status, and by remoteness — one in five
   children starts school already behind. Government has equity targets (population
   parity ambitions, a mooted universal learning entitlement) and is actively
   funding the gap. Cloud delivery, adaptive learning, and mobile access are the
   levers, and a product that demonstrably *reaches the learner the system
   struggles to reach* aligns with money and mandate at once.

These aren't niches; they're the connective tissue. The sharpest strategy in this
market is a *wedge product* aimed at a *universal pain* — narrow enough to own,
pointed at a problem every buyer is funded to solve.

## Use the rails the ecosystem already built

The last structural feature worth reading as strategy: Australia has deliberately
built **rails for EdTech entrants**, and ignoring them is leaving distribution on
the table. There's the $1.2 billion Digital Economy Strategy and concrete
infrastructure spend (the NSW fibre rollout; Victoria's A$10.8 billion education
investment and A$128 million Tech Schools Initiative). There's a dense layer of
startup support — R&D tax offsets, the Industry Growth Program, CSIRO Kick-Start,
and state-specific schemes (MVP Ventures in NSW, Ignite Ideas in Queensland,
booster grants in WA, Seed-Start in the ACT). And there's an industry spine in
**EduGrowth**, the national peak body, actively lobbying for test-bed programs and
procurement models that favour startups, plus state bodies like EdTechSA and Study
NSW running export masterclasses.

For a product strategist, that ecosystem is a map of *where adoption friction has
already been lowered for you.* Test-beds are pre-negotiated pilots. Grant programs
de-risk the build. The peak body is, in effect, lobbying to make the procurement
you're about to face easier. A company that plugs into these rails inherits
credibility and access it would otherwise spend years earning — and in a market
where the hard part is getting *into the room*, that's not a nice-to-have, it's a
core part of the entry strategy.

## What this teaches beyond Australia

Strip away the specifics and the Australian EdTech market is a clean teaching case
for any market with a complex buying structure — which is most regulated,
public-sector, or federated markets: healthcare, govtech, financial services
across jurisdictions, anything sold into education anywhere.

- **Read the market's structure before you write the product strategy.** The
  segmentation that matters is often *structural* — how the buyer is funded,
  governed, and organised — not demographic. Map the fracture lines first; they
  decide where you can actually win.
- **The funding model is a segmentation line.** Two customers who look identical
  but are paid for differently will buy differently. Match the motion to the
  money: scale-and-compliance for the system buyer, value-and-differentiation for
  the discretionary one.
- **In a fragmented market, the wedge beats the platform.** Depth in one
  defensible niche out-executes breadth across an ungovernable one. Earn the
  right to expand by owning something completely first.
- **Anchor the narrow wedge to a universal pain.** The most durable position is a
  specialist product pointed at a problem every buyer is already funded to solve.
- **Find the rails the ecosystem built.** Test-beds, grants, peak bodies, and
  infrastructure programs are a pre-drawn map of where adoption friction is
  lowest. Entry strategy is partly just knowing where the door is already open.

The headline number says Australia is one growing market. The structure says it's
a dozen of them stacked behind a single flag. The companies that win here have
stopped trying to sell to "Australian education" and started picking — a wedge, a
jurisdiction, a side of the funding line — and going deep where the market's own
shape rewards it. That discipline, far more than the product demo, is the strategy.
