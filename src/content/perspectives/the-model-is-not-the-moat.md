There is a slide in almost every EdTech pitch deck right now, and it says the
same thing: *"Now with AI."* Adaptive tutoring, AI feedback, AI-generated
practice, an AI study buddy. Australia's market is a fair sample — Mathspace has
wired in conversational LLM help, Studiosity runs AI-assisted writing feedback,
Janison and Cadmus put AI into assessment, SimConverse simulates patient
conversations for clinicians. When a capability is in everyone's deck, it has
stopped being a differentiator and become table stakes. The model layer is
commoditising in front of us.

So the interesting question about Anthropic walking into this market with *Claude
for Education* is not "is the model good?" Of course it is; so are several
others, and the gap narrows every quarter. The interesting question is the one
every product marketer should ask when their category fills up with
near-identical capability claims: **when the thing you do is no longer rare, what
is?** Read the Anthropic education play closely and the answer is unusually
clean. They're not selling the model. They're selling a **stance on pedagogy**
and a **stance on trust** — the two differentiators that don't commoditise,
because they're choices, not features.

## When the capability commoditises, the choices become the product

A model is a capability. Capabilities get matched. What doesn't get matched
easily is a *position* — a deliberate, opinionated choice about how the product
should behave, defended even when it costs you something. Positions are hard to
copy precisely because they require giving things up, and most competitors won't.

Anthropic's education positioning rests on four stated principles: *design for
true learning, ensure equitable access, protect privacy and security, provide
transparency.* It's tempting to read those as the usual values-page boilerplate.
They're not, and the tell is that each one **forecloses** something a less
disciplined competitor would happily do. "Design for true learning" rules out
the most engaging product — the instant-answer machine that maximises the feeling
of progress. "Privacy by default — we don't train on student data" rules out the
most valuable data asset in the building. Those are the marks of a real position:
you can tell what it costs.

Two of these are the load-bearing ones, and they're worth taking seriously as
competitive moats rather than marketing.

## Moat one: a pedagogy you'll actually defend

The headline is the phrase *"a thinking partner, not an answer machine."* In a
category where the default AI behaviour is to hand over the answer, Anthropic is
betting on the opposite: a **learning mode** built around Socratic questioning
that guides a student to the answer instead of supplying it, and a **Claude Code**
that pairs with a learner like a "scaled apprenticeship," showing how a
professional reasons through a problem rather than just emitting the solution.

Why does this work as a *moat* and not just a nice idea? Because it aligns the
product with the one buyer whose veto matters most and who is most sceptical of
AI: the educator. The deepest institutional anxiety about AI in education is that
it short-circuits the struggle that produces understanding — that it manufactures
the appearance of learning while hollowing out the substance. (I've argued this
exact failure mode before: an engine that optimises for "questions answered
correctly" optimises for engagement, not learning. See
[Personalising education without losing the human touch](/perspectives/personalising-education).)
A vendor that builds *against* that failure mode, and says so loudly, converts
the market's biggest objection into its sharpest differentiator.

It's also genuinely hard to copy — not technically, but organisationally. Any
competitor can add a "Socratic mode" toggle. Far fewer will hold the line when a
school district asks for the answer-machine because students rate it higher in
week one. The pedagogy is a moat to the precise extent that the company will
defend it under commercial pressure. That's the part you can't screenshot off a
landing page.

## Moat two: trust as a feature, not a footnote

The second position is quieter and, in the institutional sale, probably more
decisive: **student data is not used to train the models by default**, paired
with explicit transparency about what the system can and can't do. In a consumer
app, privacy is a footer link. In education — selling to risk-averse
institutions stewarding minors' and young adults' data — it is a primary
purchasing criterion, and increasingly a regulatory one.

Australia makes this concrete in a way that's instructive for any regulated
market. Universities there are now required to submit **AI action plans** to
TEQSA, the higher-education regulator. The sector is mid-conversation about data
governance, cybersecurity, and academic integrity all at once. In that climate,
"we don't train on your students" is not a compliance checkbox — it's the thing
that gets the product *past procurement and legal*, which is where most EdTech
sales actually die. A clean, legible trust story shortens the longest part of the
enterprise sales cycle. That is a commercial advantage measured in quarters, not
adjectives.

The deeper point for product marketers: **trust is a feature you have to build
into the product and the contract, not a tone you adopt in the copy.** You can't
message your way to it after the fact. The companies that will win the AI-in-
education decade are the ones treating data posture as architecture — and then,
yes, marketing it relentlessly, because in this category the safe choice is the
sellable one.

## Why Australia is the right place to read the strategy

A position is abstract until it meets a market. Australia is a sharp proving
ground because it's a *mature, segmented* market, not a greenfield — which means
entry strategy, not raw capability, decides the outcome. The market was around
USD 3.9 billion in 2024 and is projected to roughly double to USD 7.4 billion by
2033; it's populated by dozens of established local players, each owning a niche.
You do not walk into that market and win on "we have AI." You win by attaching
your position to the structural pains the incumbents *aren't* solving:

| Structural pain in Australian education | The position that answers it |
| --- | --- |
| Teacher shortage and workload — reports cite ~92% of teachers short of prep time, and a multi-thousand secondary-teacher shortfall | AI as a **force multiplier for teachers** (adaptive assignment generation, feedback support) — *empower*, don't replace |
| Equity gaps for low-SES, regional, and First Nations learners | "Equitable access" as a product principle — a tutor where there was none |
| A $2.2B national research-commercialisation agenda | The API as a **research accelerator**, opening doors beyond the EdTech budget line |
| Regulatory scrutiny — TEQSA AI action plans, data-governance pressure | The trust position — privacy-by-default and transparency as the entry ticket |

Notice what this reframes. The competitor in a crowded market is not the other
vendor — it's the **status quo and the institutional "no."** The winning GTM move
isn't a feature war with Mathspace or Studiosity; it's mapping each product
position onto a pain the *system* already admits it has, so the institution
experiences the purchase as relief rather than risk. That's positioning doing the
heavy lifting that capability can't.

## The discipline of entering, not storming, a market

The instinct of a strong product, walking into a fragmented market, is to compete
everywhere. The more disciplined read of the Anthropic situation is the opposite,
and it's the part I'd press hardest in a strategy review: in a segmented market
full of trusted local players with school and university relationships you don't
have, **the fastest path in is often a layer, not a frontal assault.**

Three moves follow from that, and they're general:

1. **Partner where you're a platform, not a product.** Anthropic's stated
   integrations — Canvas, Wiley, Panopto — matter more than any feature, because
   they meet institutions inside the systems they already run rather than asking
   them to switch. The same logic points to partnering with the established local
   players who own the distribution and the pedagogy-localisation, and supplying
   the reasoning layer underneath. A capability provider that insists on owning
   the whole experience in someone else's market usually loses to one that's happy
   to be the engine.
2. **Localise the position, not just the product.** A national curriculum
   implemented differently in every state, state-specific assessment regimes, an
   industry body (EduGrowth) to engage — these reward a phased, state-by-state
   entry that gathers local proof points, over a national splash. Positioning that
   doesn't survive contact with local nuance isn't a position; it's a slogan.
3. **Sell to the budget that has room.** The student-learning budget is contested
   and small. The teacher-workload problem and the research-commercialisation
   agenda are funded, urgent, and under-served. Aiming the same capability at the
   buyer with money and pain — administrators, research offices — is a
   product-marketing decision, and it's frequently the one that unlocks the
   account.

## What this teaches beyond EdTech

Strip away the sector and this is a general lesson about marketing a product in a
category the moment its core capability stops being scarce — which, in the age of
foundation models, is the situation a *lot* of products are about to find
themselves in.

- **When the capability commoditises, your positions become your product.** The
  durable differentiators are the opinionated choices you'll defend under
  pressure — on how the product should behave, and on how it should treat the
  customer's data and trust. Those don't get matched on a feature-comparison grid.
- **Trust is architecture you market, not tone you adopt.** In any regulated or
  high-stakes category, the data posture *is* the go-to-market. Build it in, then
  say it plainly and often.
- **In a crowded market, the competitor is the "no."** Point the message at the
  structural pain and the institutional objection, not at the rival's feature
  list. Win by being the relief, not the risk.
- **Enter as a layer before you storm as a suite.** Integrations and partnerships
  buy you distribution and trust you cannot build fast from scratch. Owning the
  whole experience is a luxury you earn after you're in the door, not the way you
  get through it.

The model, in the end, is the price of entry to this game — necessary, expensive,
and increasingly ordinary. What you *do* with it, who you *protect* while doing
it, and how you *enter* a market that already has its own trusted players: those
are the choices that compound. The model is not the moat. The positions you'll
defend around it are.
