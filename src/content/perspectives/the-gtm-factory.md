Every product team I've worked with eventually hits the same wall: engineering
ships faster than marketing can tell the story. Features land, but the market
narrative lags weeks behind — so adoption stalls, sales improvise, and the next
release piles on before the last one was ever properly positioned.

The usual response is to throw effort at each launch: a fresh deck, a fresh
campaign, a fresh scramble. That's bespoke go-to-market, and it doesn't scale.
The **GTM Factory** is my answer — a way to treat go-to-market as a repeatable
production system rather than a series of heroic one-offs.

## The core idea

A factory works because the inputs are standardised, the stations are known, and
the line moves at a predictable cadence. Go-to-market can work the same way if
you stop treating every launch as a blank page.

Three principles hold it together:

1. **Asset tiering** — not every feature deserves the same marketing weight.
2. **A virtual team that reaches into engineering** — the story is sourced where
   the truth lives, not invented downstream.
3. **A cadence locked to production** — marketing readiness is a release gate,
   not an afterthought.

## Asset tiering

Treating a minor enhancement and a flagship capability with the same launch
machinery wastes effort on one and starves the other. The Factory sorts every
release into tiers, and each tier has a fixed, pre-agreed asset bundle.

| Tier | Trigger | Asset bundle |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Tier 1 — Flagship** | New capability / strategic differentiator | Positioning, demo, webinar, technical article, sales talk track, champion kit |
| **Tier 2 — Notable** | Meaningful enhancement | Release narrative, demo clip, one-pager, talk track |
| **Tier 3 — Incremental** | Fixes, small uplifts | Release note, changelog entry |

Because the bundle is decided in advance, nobody re-litigates "what do we make
for this one?" every sprint. The decision collapses to a single question: *which
tier is this?*

## A virtual team embedded in engineering

The weakest go-to-market is written far from the product. The Factory runs a
**virtual team** — product marketing, a product owner, a tech lead and a
champion or two from engineering — that meets the work where it's built. This is
how you manufacture three things that decks alone never produce:

- **Product champions** who can speak to the capability with authority.
- **Thought leadership** grounded in real engineering decisions, not slogans.
- **Technical authority** the field can lean on in front of sceptical buyers.

The output isn't just collateral; it's a bench of people inside the company who
can carry the story.

## A cadence locked to production

The Factory's cadence is deliberately tied to the engineering release train. If
production has a rhythm, go-to-market inherits it. Marketing readiness for the
relevant tier becomes part of the definition of done — so the narrative ships
*with* the feature, not weeks later.

## Where AI accelerates the line

Lately I've been wiring applied AI into the Factory itself. If you can define a
**structured, intelligent description** of the product — its features,
capabilities, value propositions and GTM strategy as machine-readable inputs —
then AI can draft tiered assets, localise narratives by region, and shorten the
loop between a feature landing and the market hearing about it. The human work
moves up the value chain: editorial judgement, positioning, and deciding what's
worth saying — not first drafts.

## What it buys you

- **Time-to-market compression** — assets are pre-scoped, not invented per launch.
- **Consistency** — every release of a given tier looks and sounds coherent.
- **Resilience** — the system doesn't collapse when one person is out.
- **A growing bench** — champions and authority compound release over release.

> Go-to-market stops being a scramble and becomes a capability. That's the whole
> point of a factory: the next unit costs less than the last.

The Factory isn't anti-craft — it's what frees you to spend craft where it
matters. Standardise the predictable so you can be original where originality
actually moves the market.
